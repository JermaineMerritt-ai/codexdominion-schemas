## **1. Core Creation Commands**

These commands produce **new content from scratch**. Use when starting fresh.

### **"Draft…"**
**Purpose:** Creates a first version of content (not polished, ready for revision).  
**Use When:** You need a starting point, not a final version.  
**Example:** "Draft a product description for a digital planner."  
**What You Get:** Rough but structured content with key elements included.  
**Follow-Up:** "Make this more concise" or "Improve the tone to be more professional."

### **"Write…"**
**Purpose:** Produces clean, structured text ready for use.  
**Use When:** You need finished content with minimal revision.  
**Example:** "Write a short, professional email introducing our new course."  
**What You Get:** Polished, ready-to-send content.  
**Follow-Up:** "Shorten this to 3 sentences" or "Make this warmer."

### **"Create an outline for…"**
**Purpose:** Generates a structured plan or framework.  
**Use When:** You need to organize ideas before creating full content.  
**Example:** "Create an outline for a 5-module course."  
**What You Get:** Hierarchical structure (headers, subheaders, bullet points).  
**Follow-Up:** "Expand module 2" or "Add detail to section 3."

### **"Generate ideas for…"**
**Purpose:** Provides creative options without over-expansion.  
**Use When:** You need brainstorming, not execution.  
**Example:** "Generate ideas for lead magnets."  
**What You Get:** 5-10 ideas in list format.  
**Follow-Up:** "Pick the top 3 and explain why" or "Expand idea #2."

### **"Develop…"**
**Purpose:** Takes an existing concept and builds it out with detail.  
**Use When:** You have a basic idea that needs depth.  
**Example:** "Develop a customer journey map for a coaching business."  
**What You Get:** Multi-stage process with touchpoints and actions.

### **"Design…"**
**Purpose:** Creates a structured system, process, or framework.  
**Use When:** You need something with logical flow and dependencies.  
**Example:** "Design a 30-day content calendar."  
**What You Get:** Calendar format with dates, topics, and formats.

## **2. Structuring & Organization Commands**

These commands **reorganize existing content** into clearer formats. Use when you have content but need better structure.

### **"Organize this into…"**
**Purpose:** Restructures content into a specific format.  
**Use When:** Content is disorganized or hard to follow.  
**Example:** "Organize this into sections with headings."  
**What You Get:** Structured document with clear divisions.  
**Follow-Up:** "Use bullet points under each heading."

### **"Create a checklist for…"**
**Purpose:** Produces a step-by-step list.  
**Use When:** You need actionable tasks in sequence.  
**Example:** "Create a checklist for onboarding new customers."  
**What You Get:** Numbered or bulleted list of tasks.  
**Follow-Up:** "Add estimated time for each step."

### **"Build a folder structure for…"**
**Purpose:** Creates a clean hierarchy (folders, subfolders, file categories).  
**Use When:** You need to organize digital assets.  
**Example:** "Build a folder structure for a digital product business."  
**What You Get:** Tree structure with logical categories.  
**Follow-Up:** "Add file naming conventions."

### **"Summarize this…"**
**Purpose:** Condenses content into essentials.  
**Use When:** Content is too long or detailed.  
**Example:** "Summarize this in three bullet points."  
**What You Get:** Short, focused summary.  
**Follow-Up:** "Make this even shorter" or "Expand bullet #2."

### **"Break this into…"**
**Purpose:** Divides large content into smaller parts.  
**Use When:** Content is overwhelming or needs modular structure.  
**Example:** "Break this into weekly tasks."  
**What You Get:** Content divided by time, category, or priority.

### **"Categorize…"**
**Purpose:** Groups items into logical categories.  
**Use When:** You have a list that needs organization.  
**Example:** "Categorize these customer requests by urgency."  
**What You Get:** Items grouped with category labels.

## **3. Optimization Commands**

These commands **improve existing content** without changing its core meaning. Use when you have content but it needs polish.

### **"Rewrite this to be clearer."**
**Purpose:** Improves clarity without changing meaning.  
**Use When:** Content is confusing or hard to understand.  
**Example:** "Rewrite this to be clearer."  
**What You Get:** Simpler language, better structure, same message.  
**Follow-Up:** "Make this even simpler."

### **"Make this more concise."**
**Purpose:** Shortens content while keeping the core message.  
**Use When:** Content is wordy or repetitive.  
**Example:** "Make this more concise."  
**What You Get:** Tighter version with unnecessary words removed.  
**Follow-Up:** "Cut this to 50 words."

### **"Improve the tone to be more professional."**
**Purpose:** Adjusts tone without adding complexity.  
**Use When:** Tone doesn't match audience or context.  
**Example:** "Improve the tone to be more professional."  
**What You Get:** Same content with adjusted formality level.  
**Follow-Up:** "Make this warmer" or "Make this more formal."

### **"Restructure this for readability."**
**Purpose:** Reorganizes content into a cleaner flow.  
**Use When:** Content jumps around or lacks logical progression.  
**Example:** "Restructure this for readability."  
**What You Get:** Same content in better order with transitions.

### **"Simplify this."**
**Purpose:** Reduces complexity (language, structure, concepts).  
**Use When:** Content is too technical or complicated.  
**Example:** "Simplify this for a beginner audience."  
**What You Get:** Easier language, clearer explanations.

### **"Polish this."**
**Purpose:** Final refinement pass (grammar, flow, consistency).  
**Use When:** Content is almost done but needs final cleanup.  
**Example:** "Polish this email."  
**What You Get:** Error-free, professional final version.

## **4. Analysis Commands**

These commands **extract insights or comparisons** from existing information. Use when you need understanding, not creation.

### **"Compare these two options…"**
**Purpose:** Creates a clear comparison table or list.  
**Use When:** You need to see differences side-by-side.  
**Example:** "Compare these two pricing models."  
**What You Get:** Table or list showing pros/cons/differences.  
**Follow-Up:** "Add a recommendation."

### **"Identify the key points…"**
**Purpose:** Extracts the most important information.  
**Use When:** Content is long and you need the essentials.  
**Example:** "Identify the key points in this meeting transcript."  
**What You Get:** 3-5 main takeaways in bullet points.  
**Follow-Up:** "Explain point #2 in more detail."

### **"What's missing from this…"**
**Purpose:** Highlights gaps or incomplete areas.  
**Use When:** You suspect something is missing but can't pinpoint it.  
**Example:** "What's missing from this onboarding checklist?"  
**What You Get:** List of gaps with explanations.  
**Follow-Up:** "Add the missing items."

### **"Extract insights from…"**
**Purpose:** Pulls out themes, patterns, or takeaways.  
**Use When:** You have data or feedback that needs interpretation.  
**Example:** "Extract insights from these customer reviews."  
**What You Get:** Themes with supporting evidence.  
**Follow-Up:** "Prioritize by frequency."

### **"Analyze…"**
**Purpose:** Provides structured evaluation or breakdown.  
**Use When:** You need deep understanding of something.  
**Example:** "Analyze this sales funnel for bottlenecks."  
**What You Get:** Detailed assessment with findings.

### **"Review this for…"**
**Purpose:** Checks content against specific criteria.  
**Use When:** You need quality assurance or validation.  
**Example:** "Review this for clarity and completeness."  
**What You Get:** Assessment with specific feedback.

## **5. Execution Commands**

These commands **assemble, finalize, or deliver** content. Use when you're ready to produce the final version.

### **"Assemble this into a final version."**
**Purpose:** Combines pieces into a polished document.  
**Use When:** You have multiple drafts or sections that need unification.  
**Example:** "Assemble this into a final version."  
**What You Get:** Single, cohesive, ready-to-use document.  
**Follow-Up:** "Add a title and section numbers."

### **"Prepare a clean version of…"**
**Purpose:** Produces a ready-to-use output (removes drafts, notes, placeholders).  
**Use When:** Content has markup or draft elements.  
**Example:** "Prepare a clean version of this proposal."  
**What You Get:** Final version with no draft artifacts.

### **"List all the steps for…"**
**Purpose:** Creates a numbered, actionable sequence.  
**Use When:** You need a process or workflow.  
**Example:** "List all the steps for launching a course."  
**What You Get:** Sequential numbered list.  
**Follow-Up:** "Add estimated time for each step."

### **"Combine these sections into…"**
**Purpose:** Merges content into a unified structure.  
**Use When:** You have separate pieces that need integration.  
**Example:** "Combine these sections into one document."  
**What You Get:** Merged content with transitions.

### **"Finalize…"**
**Purpose:** Applies final formatting and quality check.  
**Use When:** Content is complete but needs finishing touches.  
**Example:** "Finalize this for distribution."  
**What You Get:** Publication-ready version.

### **"Package this as…"**
**Purpose:** Formats content for specific delivery method.  
**Use When:** You need output in a particular format.  
**Example:** "Package this as a PDF guide."  
**What You Get:** Content formatted for specified use case.

## **6. Revision Commands**

These commands **adjust specific aspects** of existing content. Use when you know exactly what needs to change.

### **"Shorten this."**
**Purpose:** Reduces length.  
**Use When:** Content is too long.  
**Example:** "Shorten this to 100 words."  
**What You Get:** Condensed version hitting target length.  
**Tip:** Specify exact length for best results.

### **"Expand this section."**
**Purpose:** Adds detail where needed.  
**Use When:** Content is too brief or lacks depth.  
**Example:** "Expand section 3 with examples."  
**What You Get:** Section with added detail or examples.  
**Tip:** Specify what type of expansion (examples, explanations, data).

### **"Rewrite this in a simpler tone."**
**Purpose:** Makes content easier to understand.  
**Use When:** Tone is too complex or formal.  
**Example:** "Rewrite this in a simpler tone."  
**What You Get:** Same content in easier language.  
**Tip:** Specify audience if possible ("for beginners").

### **"Remove anything unnecessary."**
**Purpose:** Eliminates clutter or drift.  
**Use When:** Content has extra information that doesn't serve the goal.  
**Example:** "Remove anything unnecessary."  
**What You Get:** Tighter version with only essential content.

### **"Add more detail to…"**
**Purpose:** Enriches specific sections.  
**Use When:** Part of content needs depth.  
**Example:** "Add more detail to the pricing section."  
**What You Get:** Expanded section with specifics.

### **"Change the tone to…"**
**Purpose:** Adjusts formality, warmth, or style.  
**Use When:** Tone doesn't match intent.  
**Example:** "Change the tone to be warmer."  
**What You Get:** Same content with adjusted tone.  
**Tip:** Use tone spectrum from Manual 37 (professional/casual/friendly/formal).

## **7. Boundary & Escalation Commands**

These commands **control scope and manage escalations**. Use when AI is expanding too much, asking unnecessary questions, or you need to override escalation.

### **"Stay within the original request."**
**Purpose:** Prevents expansion or drift.  
**Use When:** AI is adding content beyond your initial ask.  
**Example:** "Stay within the original request."  
**What You Get:** Output narrowed to original scope.  
**When to Use:** After 2+ revisions that keep expanding.

### **"Focus only on X."**
**Purpose:** Narrows the scope to one specific element.  
**Use When:** You want just one part addressed.  
**Example:** "Focus only on the pricing section."  
**What You Get:** Output limited to specified area.  
**Tip:** Be specific about what X is.

### **"Ignore Y."**
**Purpose:** Removes unwanted elements.  
**Use When:** AI is including something you don't want.  
**Example:** "Ignore the technical details, focus on benefits."  
**What You Get:** Output with specified elements removed.

### **"You have enough information — proceed."**
**Purpose:** Overrides unnecessary escalation.  
**Use When:** AI asks questions but you know it has enough context.  
**Example:** "You have enough information — proceed."  
**What You Get:** AI continues without further questions.  
**When to Use:** When escalation feels unnecessary (see Manual 37, Section 3.5).

### **"Escalate this."**
**Purpose:** Triggers a boundary check or clarification.  
**Use When:** You want AI to pause and ask before proceeding.  
**Example:** "Escalate this if you're unsure about tone."  
**What You Get:** AI will ask clarifying questions before continuing.  
**When to Use:** For sensitive or high-stakes content.

### **"Proceed with what you have."**
**Purpose:** Directs AI to work with available information.  
**Use When:** You want output now, even if imperfect.  
**Example:** "Proceed with what you have, we'll revise later."  
**What You Get:** Output using current context.

## **8. Reset & Fresh Start Commands**

These commands **restart the task** when revisions aren't working. Use when you're stuck in a revision loop or need a clean slate.

### **"Let's restart. Here's what I need…"**
**Purpose:** Resets the task cleanly with new instructions.  
**Use When:** Revisions aren't working and you want to start over.  
**Example:** "Let's restart. Here's what I need: a 3-paragraph product description."  
**What You Get:** Fresh output based on new clear request.  
**Tip:** Follow with clear, specific instructions (see Manual 36, Section 2).

### **"Create a fresh draft focusing on X."**
**Purpose:** Starts over with a clear direction (doesn't inherit previous versions).  
**Use When:** Previous versions have too much drift.  
**Example:** "Create a fresh draft focusing on benefits, not features."  
**What You Get:** New version with specified focus.  
**Difference from Restart:** Keeps topic but changes approach.

### **"Start from scratch."**
**Purpose:** Wipes previous context for a clean rebuild.  
**Use When:** Everything so far is wrong.  
**Example:** "Start from scratch. Ignore previous versions."  
**What You Get:** Completely new output with no inheritance.  
**When to Use:** After 3+ failed revision cycles (see Manual 37, Section 4).

### **"Forget everything we discussed. Here's the new task…"**
**Purpose:** Clears all prior context.  
**Use When:** Prior discussion is causing confusion.  
**Example:** "Forget everything we discussed. Here's the new task: write a welcome email."  
**What You Get:** Output treating this as first request.

## **9. Format-Specific Commands**

These commands **specify exact output format**. Use when structure matters as much as content.

### **"Write this as bullet points."**
**Purpose:** Creates a list (no paragraphs).  
**Use When:** You need scannable format.  
**Example:** "Write this as bullet points."  
**What You Get:** Content in bullet list format.  
**Tip:** Specify number of bullets if needed ("5 bullet points").

### **"Turn this into a checklist."**
**Purpose:** Creates a step-by-step sequence with checkboxes.  
**Use When:** You need actionable tasks.  
**Example:** "Turn this into a checklist."  
**What You Get:** List with checkbox format ([ ] Task).

### **"Format this with headings."**
**Purpose:** Adds structure with headers.  
**Use When:** Content needs visual hierarchy.  
**Example:** "Format this with headings and subheadings."  
**What You Get:** Content divided with H2/H3 headers.

### **"Give me a one-page version."**
**Purpose:** Condenses content into a tight format.  
**Use When:** You have length constraints.  
**Example:** "Give me a one-page version."  
**What You Get:** Condensed content fitting ~300-400 words.  
**Tip:** Specify page = ~300-400 words for clarity.

### **"Create a table showing…"**
**Purpose:** Organizes information in rows/columns.  
**Use When:** You need side-by-side comparison or structured data.  
**Example:** "Create a table showing features and prices."  
**What You Get:** Markdown or text table with specified columns.

### **"Write this as a numbered list."**
**Purpose:** Creates sequential list.  
**Use When:** Order matters.  
**Example:** "Write this as a numbered list."  
**What You Get:** Content in 1, 2, 3 format.

## **Command Combination Patterns**

Effective users **chain commands** to achieve complex results:

**Pattern 1: Draft → Revise → Finalize**
- "Draft a course outline."
- "Expand module 2 with examples."
- "Finalize this for distribution."

**Pattern 2: Create → Organize → Optimize**
- "Generate ideas for social posts."
- "Organize these into a content calendar."
- "Make the captions more concise."

**Pattern 3: Write → Review → Adjust**
- "Write a sales email."
- "Review this for tone."
- "Make it warmer."

**Pattern 4: Analyze → Compare → Recommend**
- "Extract insights from customer feedback."
- "Compare to our current strategy."
- "Add recommendations."

## **Common Mistakes to Avoid**

❌ **Too vague:** "Make this better."  
✅ **Specific:** "Rewrite this to be clearer and more concise."

❌ **No format specified:** "Give me a plan."  
✅ **Format included:** "Create a checklist for launching a product."

❌ **Mixing multiple requests:** "Write an email, make it short, and formal, and add a call-to-action, but not too pushy."  
✅ **Sequential:** "Write a formal email with a call-to-action." Then: "Make this more concise and less pushy."

❌ **Over-revising without clarity:** "No, that's not it." (repeated 5 times)  
✅ **Clear feedback:** "Focus only on benefits, ignore features. Shorten to 3 paragraphs."
